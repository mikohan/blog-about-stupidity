# The Collapsing Russian Economy and the Rising Threat of Hunger in 2026

As the war in Ukraine drags into its fifth year, Russia's much-touted "war economy" is showing unmistakable signs of strain. What Kremlin officials once hailed as remarkable resilience has given way to stagnation, fiscal exhaustion, and growing hardship for ordinary Russians.

## From Boom to Stagnation: The War Economy Hits Its Limits

Russia's economy defied early predictions of collapse following the 2022 invasion. Surging military production and high oil prices initially fueled growth. However, that artificial stimulus has run its course.

According to the Kiel Institute's June 2026 report, Russia's liquid sovereign wealth fund assets have plummeted from 6.5% of GDP at the start of the war to just **1.8%** by April 2026. Oil and gas revenues **collapsed by 45% year-on-year** in Q1 2026. The federal budget deficit exceeded the full-year target within just three months.

Russia's own Economic Development Ministry has sharply revised its forecasts downward. Expected GDP growth for 2026 was slashed to around **0.4%**, with analysts warning of near-stagnation or contraction persisting into 2027–2028. First-quarter 2026 data already showed a **0.2% year-on-year GDP decline**.

**Key challenges include:**

- Overheating and persistent inflation
- Severe labor shortages due to emigration and mobilization
- Depleted fiscal buffers
- Growing technological and economic dependence on China

## Soaring Food Prices and Inflation

One of the most visible impacts for Russian households is **galloping inflation**, particularly in food. A January 2026 VAT hike (from 20% to 22%) — aimed at financing the war — has accelerated price increases.

Russian media reported sharp rises in staples: cucumbers up over **21%**, tomatoes up **13.6%**, alongside increases in fuel, utilities, and basic goods. Overall inflation is hovering around **8–9%** for 2026, well above the Central Bank’s target.

While Russia remains a net food exporter, domestic challenges are mounting due to sanctions, equipment shortages, and labor deficits.

## Poverty and the Specter of Hunger

Official data shows **13.5 million Russians** living below the poverty line in 2026. Although absolute famine is unlikely, rising food prices combined with stagnant real incomes are driving increased food insecurity, especially among lower-income families, pensioners, and rural populations.

The prioritization of military spending over social programs has left vulnerable groups exposed. Complaints about unaffordable basics have become increasingly common.

## Long-Term Outlook

Russia is not in immediate freefall, but it is settling into a prolonged period of **managed decline**. Sanctions continue to raise the cost of war, while structural problems (brain drain, technological lag, and distorted resource allocation) deepen.

For ordinary Russians, this means slower growth, higher prices, and declining living standards for the foreseeable future.
